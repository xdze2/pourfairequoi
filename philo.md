# Philosophy of the project

## The core idea

**Goal: clarity. Means: structure.**

Structure is not an end in itself — it's a tool for making your current mental model legible to yourself and navigable enough to evolve it. The quality of a structure isn't "is it correct?" but "does it help you think?"

## Structure is always relative

Any structure encodes a perspective, not objective truth. Two people building a graph about the same subject — a mechanic and a driver describing a car — would produce graphs that barely overlap. Neither is wrong. Both are clarifying *for their purpose*.

This means the tool is explicitly a **first-person instrument**. The graph reflects how *you* currently think about something. It's not a universal knowledge base.

## Structure as a historical workaround

Before LLMs, computers were symbol manipulators, not meaning manipulators. To work with ideas on a computer, you had to first translate them into something a computer could handle — a tree, a graph, a schema. The structure *was* the compromise: you lost fuzziness and context, but gained queryability and traversal.

LLMs change this: language is now a first-class computational medium. But structure still buys things that language alone doesn't:

- **Navigation and orientation** — a graph gives you a place to *be*. You can ask "what's around me?" rather than searching for something you already know.
- **Explicit commitment** — naming a node and placing it is a cognitive act. Deciding what something *is* and where it *belongs* is clarifying friction.
- **Stability over time** — language is slippery; the same concept can be phrased many ways. A node with an ID is an anchor things accumulate around.
- **Gaps become visible** — in a graph, absence is visible. In a pile of text, it isn't.

So structure isn't just a workaround for computers being bad at language — it's a tool for **forcing clarity in the human mind**. LLMs don't remove the value of that discipline; they make it optional rather than mandatory.

## Structure is temporary

A structure that clarifies at the start of a project can become an obstacle later, when understanding has deepened and the original framing was too narrow or just wrong. The goal is not to build a permanent ontology but to have a structure that's *good enough now* and can be revised.

## The missing dimension

The *reason* things are connected — the point of view that makes a link meaningful — currently lives only in the author's head. If that context is lost, the structure becomes opaque. Making the perspective explicit (through node types, link types, or annotations) is an open design question.

## Roots are seeds, leaves are actions

A root node is not just "a node with no parent" — it's a **seed**: an origin, a motivating perspective, something that will grow and be transformed. It radiates outward into the graph. It gives meaning to everything below it.

A leaf node is not just "a node with no children" — it's an **atom**: a concrete action, non-splittable, something that can be resolved. It's the point where thinking stops and doing begins.

Middle nodes are neither — they're structure, connective tissue, unnamed in the visual language.

## Visual language

- `*` — root/seed. The star-like branching pattern captures the generative, radiating nature of a perspective anchor.
- `□` — leaf, todo. A square reads as a checkbox — culturally aligned with action and task.
- `■` — leaf, done. Same shape, filled — resolved, closed.
- (nothing) — middle node. Absence keeps focus on what matters.

The `*` and `□` are deliberately different shapes: one organic and radiating, one discrete and bounded. The contrast is intentional — origin and terminus are fundamentally different kinds of things.
